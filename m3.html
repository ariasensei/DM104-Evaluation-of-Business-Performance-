Core Concepts Explained

1

Ethics in Data & Performance Measurement

Simple Definition:

Ethics means doing the right thing, even when no one is watching. In data and performance measurement, ethics means collecting and using data honestly, fairly, and responsibly – protecting people's privacy and rights.

🏢 Real-World Example

Tech Company Tracking:
• Company collects user data to improve products
• Unethical: Selling data to third parties without permission
• Ethical: Using data only for stated purpose and getting clear consent

The data is the same, but HOW you use it makes all the difference.

Why Ethics Matter:

  • ✓ Protects people's privacy and rights
  • ✓ Builds trust with customers
  • ✓ Avoids legal problems
  • ✓ Creates fair business practices
  • ✓ Prevents harm to vulnerable groups
  • ✓ Ensures honest decision-making
2

Data Governance: Rules for Data

What It Is:

Data governance is the set of rules and procedures that organizations use to manage their data safely and responsibly. Think of it like traffic laws – they keep everyone safe by establishing clear rules about who can access data, how it's used, and how it's protected.

🏥 Healthcare Example

Data Governance Rules:
• Only doctors and nurses can see patient medical records
• Patient data kept for 7 years, then destroyed
• Data encrypted so hackers can't steal it
• Regular audits to check if rules are followed

These rules protect patients while allowing doctors to help them.

Key Governance Elements:

  • 📋 Access Control: Who can see and use data
  • 🔒 Security: Protection against theft/misuse
  • 📅 Retention: How long data is kept
  • 🚨 Compliance: Following laws (GDPR, privacy laws)
  • 🔍 Audit: Regular checks to ensure compliance
  • 📢 Transparency: Telling people how data is used
3

Responsible Analytics: Using Data Wisely

What It Means:

Responsible analytics means using data analysis in a way that's fair, honest, and considers the impact on people. It's about not just asking "What does the data say?" but also asking "Is it fair? Who might be harmed? Am I being honest?"

❌ Irresponsible Analytics

Problem: Company uses historical hiring data (where they hired mostly men) to build an AI that automatically rejects female applicants

Why it's wrong:
• Perpetuates past discrimination
• Treats women unfairly
• Hides bias behind "data"

✅ Responsible Analytics

Solution: Audit the data for bias, adjust it, and add human review

Why it's better:
• Reduces discrimination
• Treats candidates fairly
• Human judgment catches mistakes

Key Practices:

  • ✓ Check data for bias
  • ✓ Transparent about methods
  • ✓ Get consent from people
  • ✓ Document assumptions
  • ✓ Test for fairness
  • ✓ Keep human oversight
  • ✓ Report limitations
4

Real Risks of Misusing Data

What Can Go Wrong?

When organizations don't follow ethical guidelines and data governance, serious problems happen – harming people, the company, and society.

⚠️ Case 1: Privacy Violation - Facebook Data Breach (2018)

What happened: 87 million users' personal data was shared with a political consulting firm without consent.
Consequences: $5 billion fine, damaged trust, users felt violated.
Lesson: Companies must have strict access controls and transparency.

⚠️ Case 2: Algorithmic Bias - Amazon's Hiring AI

What happened: Amazon's AI tool trained on past hiring data automatically rejected female applicants.
Consequences: Discrimination, legal liability, public outcry.
Lesson: Data reflects historical biases; must be actively audited for fairness.

⚠️ Case 3: Data Manipulation - Wells Fargo Scandal

What happened: Employees created fake accounts to meet sales targets, using manipulated data.
Consequences: $3 billion fine, fired employees, destroyed reputation.
Lesson: Pressure to meet metrics can drive unethical behavior; need strong governance.

⚠️ Case 4: Lack of Transparency - Cambridge Analytica

What happened: Political firm used personal data for micro-targeted political ads without consent.
Consequences: Company shut down, investigations, erosion of trust.
Lesson: People deserve to know how their data is being used.

Individual Activity: Ethical Dilemma

📋 Your Situation:

You work in HR at a large company. You've discovered that the company's performance evaluation system is biased – it gives higher ratings to employees who match the demographic profile of senior managers (mostly men, aged 45+). Your boss says, "It's just how things happen naturally. The numbers show it." You have access to this data and can present it to leadership, but it might create conflict.

The Ethical Issue:

This is a fairness and transparency problem. The evaluation system is perpetuating discrimination, even if unintentionally. The question is: Do you speak up or stay silent?

Your Options:

  • A) Say nothing – not your problem
  • B) Report to leadership with data
  • C) Present to your boss first diplomatically
  • D) Leak it anonymously to an employee group

💡 Tip: There's no 'perfect' answer, but explain your ethical reasoning clearly!

👥

Group Activity: Design a Data Governance Plan

👥 Group Challenge (3-4 students):

Your retail company just experienced a data breach where customer credit card information was stolen. The CEO asks your team to create a data governance plan to prevent this from happening again and restore customer trust.

Your plan should address:

  • 1. Access Control: Who should be allowed to see customer data? Who should NOT?
  • 2. Data Security: How will you protect sensitive information from hackers?
  • 3. Retention Policy: How long should you keep customer data? When should it be deleted?
  • 4. Transparency & Communication: How will you tell customers about the breach and what you're doing to protect them?

💡 Tip: Be specific – what tools, rules, and roles will enforce your governance?

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Ethics matters: How you use data is as important as having the data itself
  • ✓ Data governance provides rules: Access control, security, retention, and transparency protect people and organizations
  • ✓ Responsible analytics requires: Checking for bias, being transparent, maintaining human oversight, and considering impact
  • ✓ Real consequences: Ethical failures lead to fines, reputation damage, legal liability, and loss of trust
  • ✓ It's your responsibility: When you see unethical data practices, speaking up is the right thing to do
